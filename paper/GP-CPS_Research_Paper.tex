\begin{abstract}
Digital twins---virtual replicas of physical systems---enable simulation, prediction, and anomaly detection without interrupting real-world operations. Building a digital twin requires learning the underlying dynamical system from multivariate sensor data, a task that becomes challenging when the dynamics are nonlinear, stochastic, and only partially observed. In this paper, we present \textbf{GP-CPS}, a framework that constructs digital twins of Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) using an ensemble of Gaussian Process (GP) regressors. Our approach learns the one-step transition dynamics of the system from data, provides principled uncertainty quantification through the GP posterior variance, and detects anomalies when observed behaviour deviates beyond $2\sigma$ from the predicted distribution. We evaluate GP-CPS on a Smart Building Zone case study with four interdependent variables (temperature, humidity, CO$_2$, occupancy) and three anomaly scenarios (heater malfunction, ventilation failure, occupancy spike). The framework achieves $R^2 > 0.95$ across all variables, with a mean absolute error of $0.23^\circ$C for temperature and 20.7~ppm for CO$_2$, while training in under 4 minutes on a single CPU core. We further analyse the learned kernel structures, uncertainty calibration, spectral properties of the GP posterior, and the sensitivity of predictions to kernel choice. Our results demonstrate that GP-based digital twins offer a computationally efficient and theoretically grounded alternative for CPS simulation, with built-in uncertainty quantification that enables reliable anomaly detection.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) integrate computation, communication, and physical processes into tightly coupled systems~\cite{lee2015,zhang2022}. The proliferation of sensors in the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) has generated vast streams of multivariate time series data that capture the complex, interdependent behaviour of these systems. This data wealth presents both an opportunity and a challenge: how can we build accurate simulators---digital twins---that replicate system behaviour without requiring explicit analytical models of the underlying physics?

Data-driven approaches have emerged as a compelling answer~\cite{yuan2019}. By learning patterns directly from sensor data, machine learning models can capture nonlinear dynamics, stochastic behaviours, and interactions between variables that would be prohibitively difficult to model analytically. Generative models, including Variational Autoencoders~\cite{kingma2013}, Generative Adversarial Networks~\cite{goodfellow2014}, and Diffusion Models~\cite{ho2020}, have been successfully applied to generate realistic multivariate time series. However, these approaches typically require significant computational resources (GPU training) and do not naturally provide uncertainty estimates for their predictions.

Gaussian Processes (GPs)~\cite{rasmussen2006} offer an alternative that addresses both limitations. As a Bayesian non-parametric framework, GPs provide:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item A principled approach to learning unknown functions from data,
\item Analytical uncertainty quantification through predictive variance,
\item A rich kernel framework for encoding structural assumptions about the underlying dynamics, and
\item Training that scales to CPU-only computation for moderate dataset sizes.
\end{enumerate}

In this paper, we introduce \textbf{GP-CPS}, a framework that leverages Gaussian Processes to build data-driven digital twins of Cyber-Physical Systems. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A GP-based modelling framework} for learning CPS dynamics from multivariate time series data, using an ensemble of independent GPs---one per output variable---with shared input features that include both current state and control inputs.
\item \textbf{Uncertainty-based anomaly detection}, where the GP's predictive variance serves as a natural confidence envelope: deviations beyond a calibrated threshold signal system anomalies without requiring a separate anomaly detection model.
\item \textbf{A comprehensive case study on a Smart Building Zone}, a multi-variable CPS with nonlinear dynamics coupling temperature, humidity, CO$_2$, and occupancy, including three distinct anomaly scenarios.
\item \textbf{Extensive analysis} of kernel selection, uncertainty calibration, spectral properties of learned kernels, and sensitivity to hyperparameters, providing practical guidance for deploying GP-based digital twins.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:problem} formulates the problem. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} reviews Gaussian Process regression. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the GP-CPS framework. Section~\ref{sec:case} describes the Smart Building Zone case study. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports experimental results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

We consider a Cyber-Physical System observed through $d$ sensor variables over discrete time steps $t = 1, \dots, T$. At each time step, the system is characterised by:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{State vector} $\mathbf{s}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$, representing the values of the $d$ sensor variables at time $t$.
\item \textbf{Control input} $\mathbf{u}_t \in \mathbb{R}^c$, representing exogenous inputs or control signals applied at time $t$.
\end{itemize}

The system evolves according to an unknown transition function $f$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = f(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{u}_t) + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t,
\qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\epsilon)
\label{eq:transition}
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t$ represents zero-mean Gaussian process noise with covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\epsilon$, capturing both intrinsic stochasticity and unmodelled dynamics. This Markovian assumption---that the next state depends only on the current state and control---is standard in system identification~\cite{ljung1999} and holds approximately when the sampling interval is sufficiently large relative to the system's time constants.

Given a dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{y}_t)\}_{t=1}^{N-1}$ of $N-1$ observed transitions, where:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_t = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{s}_t \\ \mathbf{u}_t \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{d+c}, \qquad
\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{s}_{t+1} \in \mathbb{R}^{d},
\label{eq:data}
\end{equation}

our objective is to learn a predictive model $\hat{f}$ such that:

\begin{equation}
\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) \approx \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}],
\qquad
\hat{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}) \approx \sqrt{\mathbb{V}[\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}]},
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}

where $\hat{\sigma}$ quantifies the predictive uncertainty. This uncertainty is crucial for reliable anomaly detection: when the observed next state deviates significantly from the predicted distribution, we flag a potential anomaly.

\section{Gaussian Process Regression}
\label{sec:prelim}

A Gaussian Process is a collection of random variables, any finite subset of which follows a joint Gaussian distribution~\cite{rasmussen2006}. A GP is fully specified by a mean function $m(\mathbf{x})$ and a covariance function (kernel) $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')$:

\begin{equation}
f(\mathbf{x}) \sim \mathcal{GP}(m(\mathbf{x}), k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')).
\label{eq:gp_def}
\end{equation}

For a training set $\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ with $\mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N]^\top$ and $\mathbf{y} = [y_1, \dots, y_N]^\top$, the GP predictive distribution at a test point $\mathbf{x}^*$ is Gaussian with mean and variance:

\begin{align}
\mu(\mathbf{x}^*) &= \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{y}, \label{eq:gp_mean}\\
\sigma^2(\mathbf{x}^*) &= k(\mathbf{x}^*, \mathbf{x}^*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{k}_*, \label{eq:gp_var}
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{K}_{ij} = k(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$, $\mathbf{k}_* = [k(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}^*), \dots, k(\mathbf{x}_N, \mathbf{x}^*)]^\top$, and $\sigma_n^2$ is the likelihood noise variance.

The kernel encodes our assumptions about the function being learned. We consider three widely-used kernels appropriate for physical system dynamics:

\subsection{Mat\'ern Kernel}
The Mat\'ern class with $\nu = 3/2$ assumes functions that are once-differentiable:

\begin{equation}
k_{\text{M}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \sigma^2 \left(1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}r}{\ell}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}r}{\ell}\right) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'},
\label{eq:matern}
\end{equation}

where $r = \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'\|$, $\ell$ is the length scale, $\sigma^2$ the signal variance, and $\sigma_n^2$ the noise variance.

\subsection{Squared Exponential (RBF) Kernel}
The RBF kernel assumes infinitely smooth functions:

\begin{equation}
k_{\text{RBF}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \sigma^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{r^2}{2\ell^2}\right) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'}.
\label{eq:rbf}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Rational Quadratic Kernel}
The Rational Quadratic kernel models functions at multiple length scales:

\begin{equation}
k_{\text{RQ}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \sigma^2 \left(1 + \frac{r^2}{2\alpha\ell^2}\right)^{-\alpha} + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'},
\label{eq:rq}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ controls the relative weighting of large-scale versus small-scale variations.

\subsection{Interpretation in Dynamical Systems}
In the context of learning system dynamics, the length scale $\ell$ captures the sensitivity of the next state to changes in the current state and control. A short length scale indicates that a small change in the input causes a significant change in the output---characteristic of sensitive or unstable dynamics. The signal variance $\sigma^2$ captures the overall magnitude of state changes, while the noise variance $\sigma_n^2$ quantifies the inherent stochasticity of the system.

\section{The GP-CPS Framework}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Architecture}

GP-CPS employs an ensemble of $d$ independent Gaussian Process regressors, one for each output dimension $i \in \{1, \dots, d\}$. Each GP learns the mapping from the current state-control pair to the next value of a single variable. This architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\framebox{\parbox{0.9\columnwidth}{\centering

$\mathbf{x}_t = [\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{u}_t]$\hfill
$\downarrow$\hfill\\
$\boxed{\text{GP}_1}$\hfill$\boxed{\text{GP}_2}$\hfill$\boxed{\text{GP}_d}$\\
$\downarrow$\hfill$\downarrow$\hfill$\downarrow$\\
$y_t^{(1)}$\hfill$y_t^{(2)}$\hfill$y_t^{(d)}$\\
}}
\caption{Architecture of GP-CPS. An ensemble of $d$ Gaussian Process regressors maps the current state-control pair to the next state, with each GP handling one output dimension independently.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

Formally, for each output dimension $i$:

\begin{equation}
y^{(i)} \sim \mathcal{GP}\big(m_i(\mathbf{x}), k_i(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')\big),
\label{eq:gp_ensemble}
\end{equation}

where $m_i$ is the mean function (set to zero after standardising the data) and $k_i$ is the kernel function for dimension $i$. The independence assumption across output dimensions is a pragmatic simplification that keeps computational complexity tractable: training $d$ separate GPs costs $\mathcal{O}(d \cdot N^3)$, compared to $\mathcal{O}((dN)^3)$ for a full multi-output GP. For the sizes considered here ($d=3$, $N \approx 1000$), this is manageable on CPU.

\subsection{Uncertainty-Based Anomaly Detection}

Given the GP predictive distribution for each dimension:

\begin{equation}
p(y^{(i)*} \mid \mathcal{D}, \mathbf{x}^*) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_i^*, \sigma_i^{*2}),
\label{eq:pred_dist}
\end{equation}

we define a multi-variable anomaly score:

\begin{equation}
z(\mathbf{y}^*) = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{|y^{(i)*} - \mu_i^*|}{\sigma_i^* + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:anomaly_score}
\end{equation}

where $\varepsilon$ is a small constant to prevent division by zero. An observation is flagged as anomalous when $z(\mathbf{y}^*) > \tau$, where $\tau$ is a threshold. Under the assumption that the predictive distribution is approximately correct, $z(\mathbf{y}^*)$ follows a folded normal distribution. Setting $\tau = 2$ corresponds to approximately a 95\% confidence envelope for each individual variable.

The key advantage of this approach is that it does not require a separate anomaly detection model. The GP's uncertainty naturally adapts to regions of the input space with sparse data, providing wider confidence intervals where the model is less certain.

\subsection{Multi-Step Simulation}

For forward simulation over $H$ steps, we propagate the state recursively. At step $h$, given the current state $\mathbf{s}_h$ and control $\mathbf{u}_h$, we compute:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{h+1} = \boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}_h), \qquad
\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}_{h+1} = \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}_h),
\label{eq:sim_step}
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}_h) = [\mu_1(\mathbf{x}_h), \dots, \mu_d(\mathbf{x}_h)]^\top$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}_h) = [\sigma_1(\mathbf{x}_h), \dots, \sigma_d(\mathbf{x}_h)]^\top$. The predicted state becomes the input for the next step, and uncertainty accumulates naturally through the GP predictive variances.

\subsection{Algorithm}

The complete training and inference procedure is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:gpcps}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{GP-CPS: Training and Inference}
\label{alg:gpcps}
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Training data $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{y}_t)\}_{t=1}^N$
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Kernel type $k \in \{\text{Matern}, \text{RBF}, \text{RQ}\}$
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Anomaly threshold $\tau$
\STATE
\STATE \textbf{Training}
\FOR{$i = 1$ to $d$}
\STATE Standardise $\mathbf{y}^{(i)}$ (zero mean, unit variance)
\STATE $\text{GP}_i \gets$ GaussianProcessRegressor(kernel=$k$)
\STATE $\text{GP}_i$.fit($\mathbf{X}$, $\mathbf{y}^{(i)}$)
\STATE Optimise kernel hyperparameters via log-marginal-likelihood
\ENDFOR
\STATE
\STATE \textbf{Prediction} (at test point $\mathbf{x}^*$)
\FOR{$i = 1$ to $d$}
\STATE $(\mu_i^*, \sigma_i^{*2}) \gets \text{GP}_i$.predict($\mathbf{x}^*$)
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\boldsymbol{\mu}^*$, $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^*$
\STATE
\STATE \textbf{Anomaly Detection} (at observation $\mathbf{y}^*$)
\STATE $z \gets \frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^d |y^{(i)*} - \mu_i^*| / (\sigma_i^* + \varepsilon)$
\IF{$z > \tau$}
\STATE \textbf{Flag} anomaly
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Case Study: Smart Building Zone}
\label{sec:case}

\subsection{System Description}

We model an office building zone as a Cyber-Physical System with four sensor variables and two control inputs. The system dynamics are governed by coupled differential equations capturing thermal, hygric, and air quality processes.

\subsubsection{State Variables}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $T$: Temperature ($^\circ$C), range $[15, 35]$.
\item $H$: Relative humidity (\%), range $[10, 90]$.
\item $C$: CO$_2$ concentration (ppm), range $[400, 5000]$.
\item $O$: Occupancy (number of people), discrete $\{0, 1, \dots, 5\}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Control Inputs}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $P_h$: Heater power, normalised $[0, 1]$.
\item $V$: Ventilation rate, normalised $[0, 1]$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dynamical Model}

The system evolves according to the following coupled differential equations:

\paragraph{Temperature dynamics:}
\begin{align}
\frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{1}{C_T}\Big[&\alpha P_h - \beta(T - T_{\text{ext}}) \nonumber\\
&- \gamma O(T - T_{\text{body}}) - \delta V(T - T_{\text{in}})\Big] + \epsilon_T,
\label{eq:dT}
\end{align}
where $C_T$ is the thermal capacity, $\alpha$ the heater efficiency, $\beta$ the heat loss coefficient to the exterior, $\gamma$ the heat transfer per occupant, $\delta$ the ventilation heat loss, and $\epsilon_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.05)$ process noise.

\paragraph{Humidity dynamics:}
\begin{align}
\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{1}{C_H}\Big[&\eta O(H_{\text{body}} - H) \nonumber\\
&- \theta V(H - H_{\text{ext}}) - \lambda(H - H_{\text{ideal}})\Big] + \epsilon_H,
\label{eq:dH}
\end{align}
where $C_H$ is the humidity capacity, $\eta$ the moisture production per occupant, $\theta$ the ventilation humidity exchange, and $\lambda$ the material absorption rate; $\epsilon_H \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3)$.

\paragraph{CO$_2$ dynamics:}
\begin{align}
\frac{dC}{dt} = \frac{1}{C_C}\Big[&\mu O C_{\text{body}} - \nu V(C - C_{\text{ext}})\Big] + \epsilon_C,
\label{eq:dC}
\end{align}
where $C_C$ is the CO$_2$ capacity, $\mu$ the CO$_2$ production per occupant, and $\nu$ the ventilation CO$_2$ renewal rate; $\epsilon_C \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 5)$.

\subsection{Dataset Generation}

Training data is generated by simulating 10 independent scenarios of 12 hours each (144 time steps at 5-minute resolution), with initial parameters perturbed by Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 10\%$ of nominal value) to ensure diversity. The nominal daily schedule simulates realistic office occupancy: people arrive around 8:00, a reduced occupancy during lunch (12:00--13:00), afternoon activity, and departure after 18:00. The heating and ventilation controls respond to the resulting temperature and occupancy conditions.

Validation uses 2 held-out scenarios with different random seeds. Additionally, we generate three anomaly scenarios:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Heater malfunction:} The heater is stuck at high power ($P_h = 0.9$) regardless of control signal, simulating a stuck valve or relay failure.
\item \textbf{Ventilation failure:} The ventilation rate drops to near-zero ($V = 0.02$), simulating a fan or damper failure.
\item \textbf{Occupancy spike:} A sudden influx of 5 additional occupants, simulating an unexpected event such as a meeting or gathering.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}

The experimental results demonstrate that GP-based digital twins are viable for CPS simulation. Three findings merit particular attention.

First, the high $R^2$ values ($>0.95$) across all variables suggest that the GP ensemble captures the essential dynamics despite the independence assumption between output dimensions. This is because each GP learns the mapping from the full state-control vector to a single output, implicitly modelling cross-variable dependencies through the shared input features.

Second, the uncertainty calibration analysis reveals that GP predictive variances are slightly conservative. This conservatism is desirable for anomaly detection: false alarms are preferable to missed detections in safety-critical CPS applications.

Third, the kernel analysis provides interpretable insights into system dynamics. The learned length scales quantify the temporal ``memory'' of each variable, and the noise variance estimates capture the inherent stochasticity of each sensor channel.

\subsection{Theoretical Connections}

The effectiveness of GP-based modelling for CPS dynamics can be understood through several theoretical lenses. From a Bayesian perspective, the GP posterior represents the full distribution over functions consistent with the data, providing a principled foundation for uncertainty quantification.

The connection to wide neural networks is worth noting: a single-layer neural network with an infinite number of hidden units and i.i.d. weights converges to a Gaussian Process with a specific kernel determined by the activation function and weight distribution~\cite{neal1996, lee2018}. This result, known as the Neural Network Gaussian Process (NNGP) correspondence, suggests that GP-based digital twins implicitly capture the modelling capacity of infinitely wide networks while providing exact Bayesian inference.

\subsection{Limitations}

The current framework has several limitations. First, the Markovian assumption (Equation~\ref{eq:transition}) may not hold for systems with long-range temporal dependencies or time-delayed effects. Second, the $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ scaling of GP training limits applicability to datasets beyond approximately $10^4$ points without sparse approximations. Third, the independent-GP architecture cannot capture correlations between output variables, which may be important for some applications. Fourth, the model is static and requires retraining if the system dynamics change (distribution shift).

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions for future work emerge from this study. Sparse GP approximations~\cite{quinonero2005} could scale the framework to larger datasets. Multi-output GPs~\cite{alvarez2012} would capture cross-variable correlations explicitly. Deep kernel learning~\cite{wilson2016} could automatically discover relevant features from raw sensor data. Online learning would enable adaptive digital twins that evolve with the physical system.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented GP-CPS, a Gaussian Process framework for constructing data-driven digital twins of Cyber-Physical Systems. The framework learns system dynamics from multivariate time series data using an ensemble of GP regressors, provides analytical uncertainty quantification, and detects anomalies through deviations beyond calibrated confidence bounds. Evaluated on a Smart Building Zone case study with four interdependent variables, GP-CPS achieves $R^2 > 0.95$ across all variables, trains in under 4 minutes on CPU, and demonstrates effective anomaly detection with F1 scores above 0.97.

The framework's key strengths are its computational efficiency (no GPU required), principled uncertainty quantification, and interpretable kernel analysis. These properties make GP-based digital twins particularly suitable for rapid prototyping, educational settings, and resource-constrained environments where diffusion-based or deep learning approaches are impractical.

All code, data, and experimental results are publicly available~\url{https://github.com/jcalataiut-cli/AdMaths-GP-DigitalTwin}.
